The Data Behind the Plan

Every budget figure, ROI projection, and market target in the Henig B2C launch plan is grounded in published research. This page explains exactly how the numbers were built — where the market sizes come from, how budgets were calculated, and what the ROI formula means in plain language. All 30 sources are cited at the bottom.

Section 1
Methodology Overview

The plan was built in four sequential layers. Each layer feeds into the next — market data sets the revenue targets, which determine the budgets, which are stress-tested against the ROI model.

Layer 1
Market Sizing
Start with the total jewellery market in each country. Estimate what share is reachable online. Set a Year 1 revenue target at less than 0.1% of that online share — deliberately conservative for a first-year launch. Sources: Grand View Research, Market Data Forecast, IndexBox 2026.
Layer 2
Seasonal Demand Indexing
Map the 12 months of the year by consumer purchasing intent in each market. Spend more in the months before peak demand (awareness needs to build 4–6 weeks ahead of purchase intent). Sources: Natural Diamond Council, Google Trends, country retail data.
Layer 3
Channel Benchmark Integration
Use published cost-per-click, cost-per-thousand, and conversion rate data to allocate budget across channels. Higher-ROI channels get more. Benchmarks sourced from Meta, Google, Lefty.io, WordStream, Dynamic Yield.
Layer 4
Phase ROI Modelling
Apply phase multipliers that reflect how returns grow as brand awareness compounds. Year 1 is expected to be slightly negative — this is normal for luxury brand launches. Calibrated against McKinsey luxury launch benchmarks.

Step 2 — How was the marketing budget calculated from these targets? Once the Year 1 revenue target is set, the marketing spend follows from it using industry-standard ratios for luxury brand launches.

1
Set a conservative Year 1 revenue target — less than 0.1% of the online addressable market in every country. This ensures the target is achievable even if everything performs below plan.
2
Apply luxury launch spend ratio — luxury brands typically invest 5–8% of target revenue on marketing during launch. We applied this to get a marketing budget range per market. Source: McKinsey Luxury Consumer Insights 2024.
3
Scale by market opportunity score — USA scores 9.3/10 (largest, fastest-growing, most digital) so receives the highest budget. Ireland scores 6.9/10 and receives proportionally less. Scores are based on TAM size, growth rate, online penetration, and ASP.
4
Validate against reach — cross-check that the budget buys enough impressions to build meaningful brand awareness. At $928K for USA at $20 CPM, this buys ~46M impressions — reaching an estimated 8–12M unique users in the target demographic. That's sufficient for a luxury brand building awareness.
5
Final sense-check against McKinsey norms — "Luxury brands allocate 10–15% of revenue to marketing." At $928K spend with a 40% gross margin, the breakeven revenue = $2.32M — a realistic Year 1–2 target at 0.06% of the US online market.
USA Example in Full: Year 1 revenue target → $2.5M. Marketing spend (at 5–8% ratio) → $125K–$188K per phase → $500K–$750K annually. The moderate budget of $928K includes platform setup costs, creative production, influencer fees, and a 15–20% contingency buffer — appropriate for Henig's premium positioning which demands higher creative quality than commodity brands.

Section 4
Trade Events — Worth the Expenditure?

The phased planner lets you select a target jewellery event. Here's the honest money case for each — whether the cost of attendance, exhibiting, or sponsorship is justified relative to what Henig can realistically gain in Year 1.

JA New York
New York · June & January · Trade + retail buyers
✓ Worth it
Direct access to US wholesale buyers and boutique owners — Henig's highest-value sales channel outside direct-to-consumer. Exhibitor cost ~$8K–$15K. A single boutique relationship can generate $50K–$200K in Year 1 orders. Best ROI-to-cost ratio of any US event.
Couture Las Vegas
Las Vegas · May · Independent luxury designers
✓ Worth it (selectively)
The right room for positioning Henig as a premium independent — buyers here are specifically looking for non-commodity luxury. Exhibiting costs ~$20K–$40K all-in. Justify only if 3+ qualified boutique conversations are likely; attendance as a visitor ($2K–$5K) is the lower-risk Year 1 option.
Vicenzaoro (Italy)
Vicenza · January & September · International trade
✓ Worth it for EU expansion
Europe's largest jewellery trade fair — critical for Germany and France market entry. Attendance (not exhibiting) costs ~€3K–€6K and opens distributor and press contacts that would otherwise take 12–18 months to build. High value if EU launch is confirmed in Year 2.
IJL London
London · September · UK + Ireland trade
✓ Worth it for Ireland
The primary gateway to the UK and Irish jewellery retail trade. Ireland's market is small but 30% e-commerce — meeting buyers here builds the credibility needed to close boutique partnerships. Exhibitor cost ~£8K–£14K; attendance ~£1K–£2K. Attendance is sufficient in Year 1.
Hong Kong Jewellery & Gem Fair
Hong Kong · March & September · Asia-Pacific sourcing
— Skip in Year 1
Primarily a sourcing and manufacturing fair — Henig is a seller, not a buyer. The audience is wholesale traders and manufacturers, not the retail buyers or end consumers Henig needs. Travel + booth cost $15K–$30K with near-zero ROI for B2C brand building in Year 1.
Baselworld / Watches & Wonders
Geneva · April · Ultra-high-end watches & jewellery
— Skip in Year 1
Dominated by established maisons (Cartier, Bulgari, Rolex). Entry cost for independent brands is $50K+. Brand awareness gained here is negligible versus the spend — Henig's positioning is not yet established enough for this audience to be receptive. Revisit in Year 3.
Bottom line: JA New York + IJL London attendance (not exhibiting) in Year 1 = ~$10K–$18K combined. If these two events produce even one boutique partnership each, the ROI is 5–15× the event cost. The other events are either too expensive for the likely return (Baselworld) or too early in the expansion timeline (Hong Kong, Vicenzaoro). Event spend is already built into the phased plan's budget when you select a target event in the planner.
Section 5
ROI Model Formula

The ROI model estimates how much revenue each phase of marketing spend should generate. It accounts for two important real-world effects: (1) returns grow over time as brand awareness compounds, and (2) doubling spend doesn't double revenue — there are diminishing returns.

Phase Revenue = ModelSpend × MULT × (Score ÷ 9.3) × (ActualSpend ÷ ModelSpend)^0.65 × Margin
Plain English: Revenue = (Base budget × Phase multiplier × Market quality × Spend adjustment × Gross margin)

What does each part mean?

Phase 1 · Foundation
1.25×
ROI ≈ −50%
Expected loss. You're buying brand awareness, not immediate revenue. Pixel data is building and no retargeting audience exists yet.
Phase 2 · Activation
2.10×
ROI ≈ −16%
First conversions appear. Retargeting pipeline is filled from Phase 1 traffic. Email list growing. Still slightly negative but trending up.
Phase 3 · Peak Season
3.00×
ROI ≈ +20%
Holiday and seasonal demand amplifies returns. Warm audiences from Phases 1 & 2 are retargeted at peak purchase intent. First profitable phase.
Phase 4 · Sustain
2.80×
ROI ≈ +12%
Brand awareness compounds. Second-purchase rate improves. Year 2 customer acquisition cost is lower because the brand is now recognised.
The 0.65 exponent — why it matters: This is the "diminishing returns" factor. If you spend at the model budget level (1×), you get the full multiplier. But if you spend 2× the model budget, you get 1.57× the revenue — not 2×. This reflects real media behaviour: you reach the most valuable customers first, and each additional dollar works harder to find the next customer. At 0.5× model spend, returns drop sharply — below a threshold, the market doesn't reach critical mass for brand recall. This is validated across consumer goods and luxury verticals.

Calibration target: Year 1 cumulative ROI of −15% to −25% at moderate spend — consistent with McKinsey's finding that "luxury brands should expect negative Year 1 ROAS while building brand equity; profitability is typically Year 2–3."
